# Building Models in Two Worlds: From Latent Constructs to Behavioral Signals
# 在两个世界中构建模型：从潜在构念到行为信号

Data Science Building Models in Two Worlds: From Latent Constructs to Behavioral Signals. My PhD models tried to explain why people engage. My industry models predict who will. The statistics barely changed. Everything around them did.
数据科学：在两个世界中构建模型——从潜在构念到行为信号。我的博士研究模型试图解释人们为何参与，而我的行业模型则预测谁会参与。统计学方法几乎没变，但周围的一切都变了。

Two response variables that look identical and aren’t. For my PhD, the thing I was trying to predict was a survey answer: a senior citizen’s stated intention to engage with a brand’s social media. Today, in ad measurement, the thing I predict is engagement itself, logged as a click or a purchase. Same word, near enough. But one of those variables lives only in a person’s head and has to be reconstructed from indirect evidence, and the other is sitting in a database waiting to be queried. Almost every modeling decision downstream follows from that one difference.
两个看起来相同但实则迥异的响应变量。在读博期间，我试图预测的是一项调查问卷的答案：老年人参与品牌社交媒体的意向。如今，在广告效果评估中，我预测的是参与行为本身，记录为点击或购买。词汇相同，含义相近，但其中一个变量仅存在于人的头脑中，必须通过间接证据进行重构；而另一个则静静地躺在数据库中等待查询。几乎每一个后续的建模决策都源于这一差异。

I have now built models on both sides, often with the same regression equations underneath, and what keeps surprising me is how much inverts the moment you cross from one to the other: where variables come from, what makes a variable “good,” what you are even trying to learn, and when you are allowed to stop. This piece walks through both, with code for the parts that are easier shown than described. If you have only ever modeled behavioral data, the first half is the world you skipped. If you came from social science, the second half is the one you are walking into.
我已经在两个领域都构建过模型，底层往往使用相同的回归方程。令我惊讶的是，当你从一个领域跨越到另一个领域时，一切都会发生反转：变量的来源、什么是“好”变量、你试图学习的目标，以及何时可以停止建模。本文将带你了解这两个世界，并为那些难以描述的部分提供代码示例。如果你只处理过行为数据，前半部分是你所错过的世界；如果你来自社会科学背景，后半部分则是你即将踏入的领域。

图 1. 相同的箭头形状，两个不同的世界。左侧的变量必须先构建才能使用；右侧的变量则是现成的。

The model I built then. One of my dissertation projects modeled why older adults do or don’t engage with adaptive clothing brands online. The backbone was the Technology Acceptance Model (Davis, 1989), so the predictors were constructs like perceived usefulness, perceived ease of use, brand awareness, brand interaction, brand trust, and privacy concerns, all pointing at intention to engage.
我当时构建的模型。我的博士论文项目之一是模拟老年人为何（或为何不）在网上与功能性服装品牌互动。其核心框架是技术接受模型（TAM, Davis 1989），因此预测变量包括感知有用性、感知易用性、品牌知名度、品牌互动、品牌信任和隐私顾虑等构念，所有这些都指向参与意向。

The hard part is hiding in that list. None of those predictors is a column you can select. Perceived usefulness is a state of mind. So is privacy concern. You cannot log it, and you cannot get it cleanly from one survey question either, because any single question carries its own baggage: how it is worded, whether people answer honestly, how each respondent reads it. Ask one question and you measure all of that noise along with the thing you wanted.
难点就隐藏在这个列表中。这些预测变量中没有一个是你可以直接选取的列。感知有用性是一种心理状态，隐私顾虑也是如此。你无法记录它，也无法通过单一的调查问题清晰地获取它，因为任何一个问题都带有其自身的“包袱”：措辞方式、受访者是否诚实回答、以及每个受访者如何解读它。问一个问题，你测量到的不仅是你想要的东西，还有所有这些噪音。

The standard fix is to ask several questions that circle the same construct, then let the shared variance across them stand in for the construct and treat the rest as measurement error. That buys you a new problem, though. If you just average the items and drop the average into a regression, the measurement error rides along and quietly biases your coefficients. Structural equation modeling exists largely to deal with this.
标准的解决方法是提出几个围绕同一构念的问题，然后让它们之间的共同方差代表该构念，并将剩余部分视为测量误差。然而，这又带来了新问题。如果你只是简单地对这些项取平均值并放入回归模型中，测量误差会随之而来并悄悄地偏倚你的系数。结构方程模型（SEM）的存在很大程度上就是为了解决这个问题。

Concretely, the specification for a piece of my model looked like this (using `semopy`, a Python SEM library, with synthetic variable names):
具体来说，我模型的一部分规范如下所示（使用 Python 的 SEM 库 `semopy`，变量名为合成名称）：

```python
from semopy import Model 
# `=~` defines a latent construct from its survey indicators (measurement model) 
# `~` defines a structural path between constructs 
model_spec = """ 
# measurement model: each construct built from its items 
privacy_concern =~ privacy1 + privacy2 + privacy3 
usefulness =~ useful1 + useful2 + useful3 
intention =~ intent1 + intent2 + intent3 

# structural model: what predicts intention 
intention ~ privacy_concern + usefulness 
""" 
model = Model(model_spec) 
model.fit(survey_df) 
estimates = model.inspect() 
```

That `=~` operator is the whole difference between the two worlds in one symbol. Before I can ask whether privacy concern predicts intention, I first have to build privacy concern out of `privacy1`, `privacy2`, `privacy3` and prove the construction holds. Inspecting `estimates` is where you check that: each item’s loading on its construct, plus reliability and validity metrics (Cronbach’s alpha, composite reliability, average variance extracted) that tell you whether the items really hang together.
那个 `=~` 运算符用一个符号概括了两个世界的所有差异。在我询问“隐私顾虑是否能预测参与意向”之前，我必须先通过 `privacy1`、`privacy2` 和 `privacy3` 构建出“隐私顾虑”，并证明这种构建是成立的。检查 `estimates` 就是为了验证这一点：查看每个指标在其构念上的载荷，以及信度和效度指标（如 Cronbach’s alpha、组合信度、平均方差提取量），这些指标会告诉你这些指标是否真的具有一致性。

Because every construct costs you statistical power, you cannot be generous with them. My usable sample was small, and each construct needs several indicators and enough respondents to support them. Adding a predictor is expensive. So theory, not data, decides what goes in. That is roughly the opposite reflex to “throw in the features and let regularization sort them out.”
因为每个构念都会消耗统计功效，所以你不能随意添加。我的可用样本量很小，每个构念都需要多个指标和足够的受访者来支撑。增加一个预测变量代价高昂。因此，是理论而非数据决定了模型中包含什么。这与“把特征全部扔进去，让正则化来处理”的思路截然相反。

The variable that misbehaved. Privacy concern is the one I remember best, because it refused to cooperate twice. I expected it to matter a lot. For this group, engaging with an adaptive clothing brand can mean revealing something about a disability, so on paper privacy is exactly the kind of thing that should hold people back. When I looked at the indicators, one of the three behaved differently from the others: it loaded negatively, pulling against the construct instead of with it. That item was measuring a different flavor of privacy than the other two, so I dropped it and re-estimated with the two that cohered. In ML vocabulary, I had a feature that was a bad proxy for the thing I was trying to capture, and I cut it.
表现“不听话”的变量。隐私顾虑是我印象最深的一个，因为它两次拒绝“配合”。我曾预期它非常重要。对于这个群体来说，与功能性服装品牌互动可能意味着暴露残疾相关信息，因此从理论上讲，隐私正是那种应该阻碍人们参与的因素。当我查看指标时，三个指标中的一个表现与其他不同：它的载荷为负，与构念背道而驰。该指标衡量的是与另外两个指标不同维度的隐私，所以我将其剔除，并用剩下的两个具有一致性的指标重新估计。用机器学习的术语来说，我有一个特征，它是我想捕捉的目标的糟糕代理，所以我把它删掉了。

Then the bigger surprise. In the final model, privacy concern did not significantly predict intention. Neither did brand trust. The predictors that carried the result were perceived usefulness, ease of use, brand awareness, and brand interaction. The variable I was most sure about contributed the least. I reported the null and moved on, which is the part that turned out to be good training, for reasons I will come back to.
随之而来的是更大的惊喜。在最终模型中，隐私顾虑并没有显著预测参与意向，品牌信任也没有。真正起作用的预测变量是感知有用性、易用性、品牌知名度和品牌互动。我最确信的变量贡献最小。我报告了零假设结果并继续前进，这部分经历后来被证明是很好的训练，原因我稍后会提到。
